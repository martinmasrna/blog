The Thirteen Urinal Dilemma

Picture this. You walk into a public restroom, and you're faced with a row of thirteen empty urinals. Which one do you pick?

Turns out there's a correct answer to this question. Not an approximation — the exact, mathematically optimal spot to stand. Let's get into it.

The Rules

The rules of urinal etiquette are unwritten, and yet universally known to all men:

  • Rule 1 — Never take a urinal right next to someone. No exceptions, no excuses. This one is non-negotiable.
  • Rule 2 — Otherwise, stand as far from the nearest person as you can. Give everyone as much space as possible.
  • Rule 3 — If two spots are equally far, take the one with higher number. (Farther away from the door.)

Okay. Let's see how this plays out in practice. Say there are six urinals:

Six urinals — step by step
Press Next to send in the first person.

Adam walks in first. There's nobody there yet, so Rules 1 and 2 have nothing to say — and Rule 3 just sends him straight to the back: urinal 6.

Bob comes in next. Farthest from Adam is the very front, so he takes urinal 1.

Then Carl shows up. Urinals 2 and 5 are off-limits — too close to someone. That leaves 3 and 4, which are both the same distance from the nearest person. Tie. Rule 3 settles it: urinal 4, farther from the door.

And that's it. Urinals 2, 3, and 5 are all adjacent to someone now, so Rule 1 kills them. Three people, three empty urinals, and we're done.

How full does it get?

As we can see, the room is full at just 50%. Let's give that number a name. The efficiency is how many urinals get used before nobody else can squeeze in.

\(6 \text{ urinals},\ 3 \text{ people} \implies 50\%\ \text{efficiency}\)

But try a different number of urinals, and things get strange.

  • Five urinals? Three people, 60% efficiency.
  • Six urinals? Three people, 50% efficiency.
  • Seven urinals? ... also three people, 43% efficiency.

Five, six, or seven urinals — doesn't matter, same three people fit in, only the efficiency drops. If you want, you can play around with the simulator below to see how a room fills up depending on its size:

More urinals should mean more people served ... but they don't, not always. So ... how bad can this actually get?

The best case and the worst case

For a second, forget the one-by-one arrivals. Imagine everyone spawns in the room at once. What's the absolute most people you could ever fit? Easy — fill every other urinal. That's about half the room, rounded up, and you physically can't do better. Any fuller and two people are standing shoulder to shoulder.

So, half is the hard ceiling, the absolute best case. What about the worst case?

The worst the etiquette can leave you with is people spaced three urinals apart — two empty gaps between every pair. Only about a third of the room (~34%) gets used.

Now, the question is: Are there specific restroom sizes that always hit the ceiling? And sizes that always hit the floor?

Yes. And they have a pattern — look for the green dots in the graph above.

The blessed sizes — the ones that always fill to the max — are:

3, 5, 9, 17, 33, …

Each number is just double the previous, minus one: 3 → 5 → 9 → 17 → 33.

Why these sizes are optimal (warning: math inside)

We claim that all \(N = 2^k + 1\) (numbers 1 greater than some power of two) are optimal. Let's prove it:

After the first two people claim the ends, there is one gap (distance between two occupied urinals) of \(N - 1 = (2^k + 1) - 1 = 2^k\). Then, each new arrival fills the midpoint of the widest existing gap, splitting it into two equal halves of length \(2^{k-1}\).

This works cleanly at every step because the gap distance is always a power of 2 — and powers of 2 are always even, so the midpoint is always a whole number and the split is always perfectly equal, all the way down to gaps of length \(2^1 = 2\). At which point every other urinal is occupied (more specifically, \(\lceil n/2 \rceil\) urinals) and the optimal efficiency has been achieved.

And the cursed sizes — the ones that always sink to the worst case (red dots on the graph):

4, 7, 13, 25, 49, …

Again, we double the number and subtract 1: 4 → 7 → 13 → 25 → 49.

Why these sizes are worst-case (warning: math inside)

We claim that all \(N = 3 \cdot 2^k + 1\) are worst-case. Let's prove it:

After the first two people claim the ends, there is one gap of \(N - 1 = 3 \cdot 2^k\). Each new arrival fills the midpoint of the widest gap, splitting it into two equal halves of length \(3 \cdot 2^{k-1}\).

Again, this halving works cleanly at every step because \(3 \cdot 2^k\) is always even. But this time the process ends at gaps of length \(3 \cdot 2^0 = 3\), not 2. A gap of distance 3 contains two empty urinals — but both are directly adjacent to a neighbor, so Rule 1 disallows both of them. Nobody else can squeeze in, and the room bottoms out at ~34% efficiency.

So, genuine public service announcement for anyone building a restroom: install 3, 5, 9, 17, or 33 urinals. If you can't do that, at least avoid even numbers. And never, ever, under any circumstances install 4, 7, 13, or 25 urinals. I'm serious.

Saving the room

Right. So thirteen is one of the cursed numbers, which brings us back to where we started.

Left on autopilot, this room serves five people when it could fit seven. Two strangers will walk in later, find nowhere to stand, and have to leave. And it's partly your fault — you pick first, and your spot sets the whole thing in motion.

Can you actually fix this?

Where do you stand?
Click any urinal to place yourself.

The obvious move is to stand dead center — urinal 7. You're splitting the room into two halves of five urinals each, and five is a blessed number, so ... it should work, right?

It doesn't. The two "halves" were never actually separate — everyone is still spacing off you and off each other. The room settles back into the same sparse five-person pattern it was cursed with.

So, what is the solution?

Turns out, the move that works is the one that looks wrong ... or at least weird. Don't stand at either end, and don't stand in the middle. Stand at urinal 9.

The room is now lopsided, and that's exactly the trick. The next people fill in at 1, then 13, then 5 — suddenly you've got people at 1, 5, 9, and 13, evenly spaced every four urinals. Each of those three gaps takes exactly one more person — at 11, 7, and 3. Seven people served — the best-case ceiling of ~50% we saw earlier, and the absolute maximum for any 13-urinal room. You turned the worst possible room into a perfect one, and nobody else had to do anything differently.

Conclusion

So, what's the solution to the thirteen urinal dilemma?

As it turns out, just one person thinking past themselves turns the worst-case scenario into a perfect one. Everyone else acts "selfishly" — and yet, somehow, the room fills out perfectly.

Anyway. Next time you walk into a restroom, you now know more about it than you'll ever need to. And who knows, you might just save the room.
